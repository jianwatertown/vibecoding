This is the cash you actually receive (or pay out of pocket if negative) each year.
Total Cash Flow (simple)Σ Annual CF for all years. Does not account for time value.
Total Cash Flow (compounded)Σ (Annual CFy × (1 + Risk-Free Rate)^(N − y))
Each year's negative CF is compounded forward to the end of the hold period at the risk-free rate (e.g., 4% US 10yr bond). This shows the true opportunity cost: money spent subsidizing the property could have been earning 4%/yr instead.

Why does cash flow change each year? Rent grows at Rent Growth % while mortgage payments are fixed. Expenses grow at Expense Growth %. If rent grows faster than expenses, cash flow improves. If Expense Growth = 0% (flat expenses), the model is optimistic — in reality, property tax and insurance typically increase 2–3%/yr.

Each month, as the balance decreases, more of your payment goes to principal and less to interest. Over the hold period, this "principal paydown" builds equity even when cash flow is negative.

IRR (Internal Rate of Return) — Primary Return Metric

MetricFormula
Initial EquityOption 1 "Net Cash to Owner" = Sale Price − All Selling Costs − Mortgage Payoff
This is the actual cash you forgo by not selling today. Using raw Market Value − Mortgage would overstate your equity because it ignores the 7–9% in selling costs you'd pay to realize it.
IRR Cash FlowsYear 0: −Initial Equity (opportunity cost of not selling)
Year 1..N−1: Annual Cash Flow for that year
Year N: Annual Cash Flow + Net Exit Proceeds
IRRSolve for rate r where: Σ CFt / (1+r)t = 0
This is the discount rate that makes the net present value of all cash flows equal to zero.

Why IRR instead of Simple ROI?

Simple ROI = (Total Profit) / (Initial Equity) ignores when cash flows occur. If you lose $25K/yr for 5 years, that money could have earned ~4% elsewhere. IRR accounts for this by discounting each year's cash flow. A negative annual cash flow is penalized more heavily in early years because it compounds over time.

Example: If IRR = 2.5% and your alternative investment earns 4%, the rental is destroying value — you'd be better off selling and investing the equity elsewhere.

JV vs Rental IRR: Both options require you to keep paying the mortgage. JV eliminates maintenance/tax/insurance costs but also eliminates rent income. The payoff comes from the development profit split. JV typically has higher IRR due to larger payout over a shorter timeframe, but carries significantly more risk (construction delays, cost overruns, market timing).

Key Concepts & Glossary

TermDefinition
Forward AppreciationThe increase in property value from today onwards only. Calculated as: (Current Value × (1 + Rate)^Years) − Current Value. Past appreciation is already embedded in today's market value and is not counted as a return. If the property is worth $1.5M today and grows 3%/yr for 5 years, forward appreciation = $1.5M × (1.03⁵ − 1) = ~$244K.
Initial EquityThe actual cash you'd receive if you sold today (Option 1's "Net Cash to Owner"). Equals Sale Price − Selling Costs − Mortgage. This is the true opportunity cost — not the theoretical Market Value − Mortgage, which ignores the ~8% selling costs needed to liquidate.
NOINet Operating Income. Rental income after all operating expenses but before mortgage payments. The property-level profitability before financing.
Cap RateNOI ÷ Market Value. A financing-independent measure of property yield. Typical for Bellevue SFH: 1.5–3%.
IRRInternal Rate of Return. The annualized return that accounts for the timing and size of every cash flow. Unlike simple ROI which just divides total profit by investment, IRR penalizes early negative cash flows because that money could have been earning returns elsewhere.
Risk-Free RateThe return you could earn with zero risk (typically US 10-year Treasury bond, ~4% in 2025). Used to calculate the true opportunity cost of negative cash flows: each dollar you feed into the property could instead be earning this rate risk-free.
Expense Growth RateAnnual increase in operating expenses (property tax, insurance, maintenance, utilities). 0% = flat (optimistic). 2–3% reflects typical real-world escalation. PM fee is already a % of rent and thus grows automatically with rent.
LTCLoan-to-Cost. Construction loan amount ÷ total project cost. Lenders typically cap at 60–75%.
WSSTWashington State Sales Tax applied to construction costs. Currently 10.2% for Bellevue.
GC FeeGeneral Contractor management fee, typically 10–15% of hard construction costs.
Preferred ReturnA hurdle rate on invested capital that must be paid before profit splitting begins. Protects the capital contributor.
WaterfallThe sequential order in which sale proceeds are distributed: first repay debt, then return capital, then preferred returns, then profit split.
